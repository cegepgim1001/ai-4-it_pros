Lecture Overview

Good evening, everyone. Welcome to our session on Generative AI and Prompt Engineering. Today is January 29, 2026.

I know many of you have been hearing the buzz‚Äîor perhaps the noise‚Äîabout AI for years now. We are past the initial hype cycle of 2023 and 2024. In 2026, AI is no longer a "magical toy"; it is digital infrastructure, much like the cloud became a decade ago. We aren't here to discuss sci-fi; we are here to discuss utility, automation, and how to keep your jobs relevant in an era where Agents and Reasoning Models are writing code and deploying infrastructure.

Lesson 1: Introduction to Generative AI

1. The 2026 Reality Check: What is AI?

Let's cut through the marketing fluff. AI (Artificial Intelligence) is the broad discipline of creating machines that can perform tasks requiring human intelligence. ML (Machine Learning) is a subset where machines learn from data rather than being explicitly programmed with rules. GenAI (Generative AI) is the subset that *creates* new data (text, code, images, audio, 3D models) rather than just analyzing existing data.

In 2026, we have moved beyond simple "Chatbots." We are now in the era of Agentic AI‚Äîsystems that don't just talk back but do things (execute code, browse the web, manage Kubernetes clusters).

Real-Life Example: The "Self-Healing" Server

Task: Comparing a traditional script to a 2026 AI Agent.
Relation to Lecture: Illustrates the shift from "Explicit Programming" to "Generative Reasoning."

  • Old Way (Rule-Based AI/Scripting): You write a Bash script that checks if CPU > 90%. If yes, it restarts the service. If the error is something you didn't predict (e.g., a memory leak causing a deadlock), the script fails.
  • New Way (Generative AI Agent): An AI agent monitors the logs. It notices latency. It generates a hypothesis: "This looks like a database lock." It generates a query to check the lock. It decidesto kill the specific process. It writes a post-mortem report for you.

2. How It Works: Transformers & Tokens

Under the hood, these models (GPT-4, Gemini, Llama 4) are built on the Transformer architecture. They don't "know" facts; they predict the next "token" (roughly a word or part of a word) based on probability. Think of it as Autocomplete on Steroids.

3. IT Use Cases: The "Boring" but Powerful Stuff

In IT, we don't need AI to write poetry. We need it to handle the drudgery.

  • Code Generation & Refactoring: Updating Python 2 to Python 3, or Java 8 to 21.
  • Documentation (Docs-as-Code): Automatically generating `README.md` files from code comments.
  • Security Analysis: Parsing terabytes of logs to find anomalies that Regex patterns miss.

4. Risks: Hallucinations & Security

Hallucination: When the AI speaks confidently but lies. Data Leakage: Never paste your company's AWS Private Keys or customer PII (Personal Identifiable Information) into a public LLM.

Lesson 2: Prompt Engineering

5. What is Prompt Engineering?

Prompt Engineering is not just "asking questions." It is programming in prose. It is the skill of designing inputs (prompts) to get optimal outputs from Generative AI models. In 2026, we define it as "Model Steering."

Real-Life Example: The "Intern" Analogy

Treat the AI like a highly intelligent, extremely literal summer intern who has read every book in the world but has zero context about your company.

  • Bad Prompt: "Write an email about the outage."
  • Good Prompt: "Draft an email to 'All Staff' regarding the 15-minute email outage this morning. Tone: Apologetic but professional. Mention that no data was lost. Sign it from the IT Director."

6. Techniques: Zero-Shot, Few-Shot, Chain of Thought

  • Zero-Shot: Asking without examples. (Often fails for complex tasks).
  • Few-Shot: Providing examples in the prompt. (The "Do as I do" method).
  • Chain of Thought (CoT): Asking the AI to "think step-by-step." This forces the model to generate reasoning tokens before the final answer.

7. Parameters: Temperature & Tokens

Even in 2026, we tune these:

  • Temperature (0.0 - 2.0): Controls randomness.
    • 0.0: Deterministic. Good for Code, SQL, Math.
    • 1.0: Creative. Good for Brainstorming, Marketing.
  • Max Tokens: Limits the output length.
